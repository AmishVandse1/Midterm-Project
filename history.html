Operational History

The Boeing 747-100 was the original variant of the Boeing 747, rolling out of the Everett factory plant in 1968. The -100 was entered into service with Pan American World Airways. The aircraft offered airlines an increase in passenger capacity, speed, and range, resulting in an order count of 168. Following the -100, Boeing would go on the produce the -200, -300, -400, -8i, and many other variants, amassing a total order count of 1,574. The 747 platform also became the go-to cargo aircraft for airlines like UPS, Cargolux, and Cathay Pacific Cargo. Due to the COVID-19 pandemic and the availability of more fuel-efficient aircraft, the Boeing 747 is quickly being phased out of the skies and being replaced by more efficient twin-engine jets, marking the end of an era.

Design

The Boeing 747 is a large, wide-body aircraft that carries four wing-mounted engines. The aircraft features a distinct hump at the top, allowing for more seating and being the first-ever double-decker passenger airplane. It was originally designed with a seating capacity of 366 passengers in a standard 3-4-3 configuration. However, this number can change depending on the number of J and F class seats. The Boeing 747 has a maximum takeoff weight of 735,000 pounds to 970,000 pounds. Newer versions of the 747, such as the 747-8i, have a nautical range of 8,000 miles, enough to go from New York to Hong Kong. The 747 features four redundant hydraulic systems and four main landing gears. The aircraft also features a split control surface designed with triple-slotted flaps to minimize landing speed.
